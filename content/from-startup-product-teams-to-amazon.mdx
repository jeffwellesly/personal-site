Nine years into a product career, the biggest shift in how I think about the job didn't come from a course or a book. It came from working in two environments that reward almost opposite instincts. Early in my career, I worked with founding teams building 0-to-1 products, including scaling a K-12 live learning platform from zero to more than 6 million users, work that contributed to over $100 million in revenue impact. Today, I'm at Amazon, driving product strategy for Cross-Border Delivery Experience, a mature, high-scale operation with a completely different set of constraints. Both experiences shaped how I do the job. Neither one alone would have been enough.

## What startup product work teaches you

Early-stage product work is defined by ambiguity you can't spec your way out of. There's no existing system to reference, no established user base to survey at scale, and often no clear precedent for the decision in front of you. You make calls with incomplete information because waiting for complete information means someone else ships first, or the company runs out of runway before you find out you were right.

Speed compounds with this ambiguity. On a founding team, the distance between "we should try this" and "users are using this" is measured in days, not quarters. That closeness to the customer is the real advantage: when something isn't working, you hear about it directly, often within a day, not through a research report three sprints later. Scaling that live learning platform from a standing start to 6 million users meant constantly rebuilding the product around what we were learning in near real time, because the alternative, waiting for a formal process to validate a hypothesis, was slower than the market.

The tradeoff is that everything is fragile. Systems break under load you didn't anticipate. Processes that worked for ten thousand users don't survive contact with a million. You are always slightly behind the scale you're actually operating at, and you learn to build for "good enough to hold until we can fix it properly" rather than for permanence.

## What operating at Amazon's scale teaches you

Amazon is the opposite environment in almost every dimension that matters. Cross-Border Delivery Experience touches customers, carriers, and internal systems across many countries, which means a change that looks simple in a design doc has to be evaluated against dozens of edge cases, existing systems, and stakeholder groups before it ships anywhere. Speed still matters, but it's speed within a system built to not break, because at this scale, an unconsidered change doesn't cause a minor bug, it causes a customer-facing incident across a meaningful chunk of a global operation.

The core skill this environment demands is stakeholder alignment as a form of engineering, not a soft skill you tolerate. Getting a cross-border delivery experience change shipped means understanding how it affects logistics, engineering across multiple systems, legal and compliance in different countries, and other product teams whose roadmaps intersect with yours. The work isn't just deciding what's right. It's building the case and the coalition to make the right thing actually happen inside a large, interdependent system. And because the systems involved are long-lived and heavily used, you have to think in terms of years, not sprints. A shortcut that saves a week now can cost months later if it doesn't hold up at the scale and lifespan this kind of system operates at.

## What stayed constant

Underneath both environments, the actual job hasn't changed: figure out what the user or customer actually needs, figure out what's true about the system you're working within, and make the smallest, clearest decision that moves both forward. What changes is the cost of being wrong, the speed at which you find out, and who else you need to bring along to execute well.

My MBA from the University of Washington's Foster School of Business sits between these two chapters, and it's where I formalized a lot of what I'd learned by instinct in the startup years: how to reason about a business model, not just a feature; how to read a market, not just a user. I use that structure daily now, applied to a scale I hadn't operated at before.

This is the honest version of my range: I've built something from nothing under real ambiguity, and I've learned to operate inside a system too large and consequential to move by instinct alone. Most roles need some mix of both, and knowing which instincts to reach for depending on the problem in front of me is the thing nine years has actually taught me.

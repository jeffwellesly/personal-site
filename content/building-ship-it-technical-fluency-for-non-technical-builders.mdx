Most people who want to work well with engineers eventually run into the same wall. Glossaries and "explain like I'm five" articles get you vocabulary, but not judgment. Bootcamps get you real skills, but they're built for people who want to become engineers, not for people who want to understand engineering well enough to do their own job better. I kept hearing a version of this problem from PMs, founders, and MBA classmates: they didn't want a new career, they wanted to stop feeling like they were guessing.

I built Ship It because I ran into this gap myself, and because it maps almost exactly onto problems I've solved before.

## The problem with how technical fluency is usually taught

There are really two products on the market today. One is shallow: a glossary, a "no-code" explainer, a listicle titled "APIs explained simply." You finish it able to define a term in a meeting, but not able to reason about why an engineer is pushing back on your timeline. The other is deep: a full bootcamp, weeks of curriculum, assuming you want to write production code for a living. Most non-technical builders don't want either. They want to understand how the pieces fit together — enough to ask a sharper question, scope a feature more realistically, or build a small project themselves — without quitting their job to do it.

That gap is where Ship It lives.

## Who this is actually for

Ship It is built for PMs, founders, MBA students, and career switchers — people who already have judgment and context, but not a mental model for how software and AI products actually get built. Some want to be better partners to their engineering teams. Some want to prototype an idea without waiting on anyone. Some are switching careers and need proof they can build, not just talk about building. The common thread is that they learn best by doing, not by reading definitions.

## How Ship It is structured

The product is organized around tracks — software development and AI/ML — broken into lessons that explain a concept in plain language before asking you to use it. You learn what an API is, then you call one. You learn what a database is, then you set one up in Supabase. You learn what deployment means, then you ship something to Vercel with GitHub. The lessons build on each other, and progress is visible, which matters more than it sounds — the biggest reason people quit self-directed technical learning isn't difficulty, it's the absence of any sense that they're getting somewhere. A structured, visible sense of progress is what turns "I've been meaning to learn this" into something people actually finish.

The content also has to be honest about tradeoffs, not just mechanics. Understanding what Supabase does is less useful than understanding when you'd reach for it instead of building your own backend, and why that choice matters for a small team's velocity.

## What building this shows about how I work

Ship It isn't a side project I bolted onto a resume — it's the same kind of work I've done professionally, just in public and end to end. A few things about how I approached it are worth calling out directly:

- **Product discovery.** The idea didn't start from "wouldn't it be cool if." It started from a recurring, specific complaint I heard from people I respect, which I then tested against my own experience before writing a line of curriculum.
- **User empathy.** Non-technical builders don't need less information, they need better-sequenced information. Getting the sequencing right — what to explain before what to build — took more iteration than any single lesson's content.
- **Curriculum design.** Teaching something well enough that a non-technical person can apply it immediately is a different skill from knowing the subject yourself. It's closer to instructional design than to writing documentation.
- **Shipping.** Ship It is live. It's not a deck or a prototype behind a login wall — it's a real product a stranger can use today, which is the same bar I hold my professional work to.

## Where it goes from here

The version live today covers the fundamentals: APIs, databases, deployment, GitHub, and the basics of AI/ML, taught through building rather than reading about building. The next tracks will go deeper into the product-building side — how to take a working prototype and think about it as an actual product, not just a technical exercise. That's the part of the gap I think is least served right now, and it's the part I'm most qualified to build.
